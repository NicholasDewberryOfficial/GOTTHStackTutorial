I started my Go journey in Spring 2024, and I started making web apps in Summer 2024. Before then it was necessary to use a framework (such as Fiber or Chi) to do anything web-related.




These frameworks were extremely well-written and had many QOL features that made them from stand out from one another, as well as special use cases that they all covered well.

However - in 2025, and for most people, I reccomend against using a web framework. Net/Http has come a long way (It got REALLY good in Spring 2024) and you don't need all of the bloat that comes from a typical web framework. They also solve problems that are really valuable for you to solve yourself. For example, CSRF attacks are pretty common - but you can use a middleware like gorilla/mux to highlight specific portions rather than just relying on built-in functionality. Also, writing boilerplate code might be inelegant - but it helps you to understand what's going on at a granular level.

Phlisophically, Idiomatic Go prefers limiting the use of imports - especially those that are from outside of the default Go registy. It helps to make building and debugging easier, and it just makes things simpler in the long run.

With that being said, there are a ton of benefits to using frameworks. For one, the documentation is much better - but it fundementally comes down to what you want. Are you someone who's learning to sharpen their skills in Go? Then I don't reccomend it. Up against a deadline, and you need a batteries included solution? You might want to consider Chi.

Thankfully, a lot of the skills that you get from net/http and frameworks are extremely transferrable, and you can change your mind whenever you like. (click me) Here's a Fall 2024 blog discussing the differences in routers.
